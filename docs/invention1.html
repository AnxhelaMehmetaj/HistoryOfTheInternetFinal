The Internet is the next big thing!

Overview

"The Internet is the next big thing. Its on par with the wheel, fire, language, the printing press” begins this documentary from the History Channel talking about how the Internet is just as important and revolutionary as the wheel or the printing press.


There is no doubt that the Internet has been a revolutionary invention, but how did it begin? To begin with, we will start with computers since they are the objects needed to access the Internet. During the Space war, the US government noticed how the Soviet Union was advancing quicker than them to space. Due to the US government not wanting to fall behind in space exploration, President Eisenhower created a Bureau at the Defense department called the Advance Research Projects Agency, mainly known as ARPA. ARPA would take care of space exploration and computer science research. Soon NASA took over and left computer research behind. In the earliest form of computers, computers could take up the space of an entire room. There were also very few of these computers since they were expensive and costly to maintain. As time went on, millions of dollars went into research of computers and networks. One notable form of network or communication that came from this would be that no matter if a line of communication between two computers was cut, there would be vast other options for the data to get from computer A to computer B. At some point many institutions were creating their own computers that contained their own protocols and if anyone else wanted to connect to them they would have to follow their same protocol. This wasn’t very efficient as now every computer had to be programmed the protocol of another computer before connecting to it and wasn’t very scalable. The creation of the ARPAnet was the answer to this problem, also known as the ARPA network. The ARPAnet connected all computers in its network to each other making scalability possible. However, using the ARPAnet was very esoteric and not many people knew how to use it. A couple years later, Tim Berners Lee, created the World Wide Web allowing regular people to connect to the ARPAnet. This was when the Internet as we know it today took off.

At MIT the graduate student named Leonard Kleinrock begin applying Queueing theory to date to transmission. queue is a very simple structure. It's simply a system to you which arrives hanging around while and then leave. Like letters going through of posts' offices, kleinrock shows how packages of data would queue or line up at the communication network as they transited through.This way he could determine the overall speed of the network. He then showed how to speed up the internet by implementing the two techniques: demand access and distributed control. So the point of the demand access, you dont get it till you need is the key idea for effective resource sharing with data network. The second principal that he uncovered was the idea to use distributed control in network. Noone switch device authority should control the entire network, rather every switch should sharing that control. It should decide the routing procedure, decide how much traffic was allowed in, where it's going, when to do reassembly, when to do all the function in redistributed fashion.
